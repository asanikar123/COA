About City of Asylum

A grassroots safe haven, built by community.

Founded in 2004 by two Pittsburgh residents, City of Asylum grew from a single act of courage into the world's largest program for persecuted writers — powered entirely by individuals and foundations, not governments.

Our Mission

Protecting and Celebrating Freedom of Creative Expression

City of Asylum builds a just community through three interconnected commitments.

Provide Safe Haven

We give endangered writers and artists a safe home so they can continue to create and their voices are not silenced. For two years or more, residents receive housing, stipends, legal counsel, and medical benefits.

Celebrate Creative Expression

We offer a broad range of free literary, arts, and humanities programs in a community setting to build social equity through cultural exchange — over 200 programs each year.

Transform Neighborhoods

By converting dilapidated properties into homes for our programs and writers, we anchor neighborhood economic development on Pittsburgh's Northside.

Our Founders

Diane Samuels & Henry Reese

In 1997, Salman Rushdie gave a talk in Pittsburgh and briefly mentioned the Cities of Asylum network — European cities that protected writers whose lives were in danger. Diane Samuels and Henry Reese were in the audience, and they were immediately drawn to this mission.

"Most endangered writers come from circumstances where they have neither fame nor resources. We wanted to change that."

They wrote to Cities of Asylum in Europe about starting a chapter in Pittsburgh. Getting no response, they kept writing — until in 2003 they connected with author Russell Banks, who was expanding the network to the U.S. In 2004, they welcomed Huang Xiang as their first writer.

Our History

20 Years of Safe Haven

1997

A Spark in the Audience

Diane Samuels and Henry Reese attend a Salman Rushdie talk in Pittsburgh. He mentions the Cities of Asylum network in Europe, and an idea is born.

2004

First Writer Arrives

Huang Xiang, China's pre-eminent post-Cultural Revolution poet, arrives as the first writer-in-residence. He creates "House Poem" on the facade of 408 Sampsonia Way.

2006

A Growing Safe Haven

Additional writers arrive from around the world. The Sampsonia Way neighborhood begins its transformation into a literary corridor.

2018

Alphabet City Opens

City of Asylum opens its permanent home at 40 W. North Avenue — Alphabet City — a cultural hub with a stage, bookstore, and restaurant.

2022

Ukrainian Fellowship Launched

In response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine, City of Asylum creates a special fellowship bringing three Ukrainian writers to Pittsburgh.

2026

18 Writers and Growing

Today, City of Asylum has hosted 18 writers from 15+ countries — nearly 10% of all exiled writers in the 80-city International Cities of Refuge Network.

What Makes Us Different

A Model Like No Other

City of Asylum operates differently from every other safe haven in the world.

🏠

Long-Term, Not Temporary

Most cities of asylum provide emergency relief for a year. We commit to two years or more — helping writers build a permanent new life.

🌱

Grassroots, Not Government

We are the only program in the world not funded by governments or universities. We are entirely community-supported.

Supported by the Ford Foundation, Heinz Endowments, the Pittsburgh Foundation, the NEA, and 11 other foundations.

📖

Every Writer Creates

Every writer or artist hosted has created a full-length work while in residence — and has been able to continue creating after.

🤝

Community Integration

Writers become part of Pittsburgh's fabric through university partnerships, school visits, festivals, and free public programming.
