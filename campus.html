The Campus

Seven blocks transformed

It started with one poet painting his words on a house. Now it's a neighborhood.

How It Grew

From One House to a Neighborhood

2004

The First House Poem

Huang Xiang paints his "House Poem" — the first house publication on Sampsonia Way. A single act of creative defiance that would define an entire neighborhood.

2014

River of Words

140 neighbors mount words on their homes. River of Words stretches half a mile across the Northside, making the entire community a canvas for free expression.

2015

Alphabet Reading Garden

A nuisance bar becomes the Alphabet Reading Garden — a public space with plants A to Z and the alphabet in 70 writing systems.

2017

Alphabet City Opens

Alphabet City opens at 40 W. North Ave. — a cultural hub with a performance space, bookstore, and Cucina Alfabeto restaurant.

2022

Seven Writer Residences

City of Asylum now hosts 7 writer families at once — the largest sanctuary for persecuted writers anywhere in the world.

What's Here Now

A Campus Built for Creative Freedom

Four interconnected spaces across Pittsburgh's Central Northside, all open to the public.

Alphabet City

40 W. North Ave.

The cultural heart of the campus — a performance space, independent bookstore, and the Cucina Alfabeto restaurant, all under one roof. Available for private event rental (35 to 100 guests).

Performance space Bookstore Restaurant Event rental

Sampsonia Way House Publications

308 – 408 Sampsonia Way

A walkable outdoor gallery featuring 7 large-scale collaborations between exiled writers and visual artists. Each house facade tells a story of resilience and creative freedom.

Outdoor gallery 7 collaborations Writer & artist

Alphabet Reading Garden

3 blocks from house publications

A public green space where a nuisance bar once stood. Plants from A to Z, the alphabet rendered in 70 writing systems, and a quiet place for reading and reflection.

Plants A–Z 70 writing systems Public park

River of Words

Across 140 Northside homes

140 words mounted on 140 homes across the Northside, stretching half a mile. Created by Venezuelan writer Israel Centeno in 2014, it is the largest community-authored public art project in Pittsburgh.
