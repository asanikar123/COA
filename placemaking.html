Placemaking

Art Transforms Places. Places Transform Lives.

City of Asylum reimagines Pittsburgh's Central Northside through art-driven placemaking — turning ordinary homes into house publications, streets into galleries, and a neighborhood into a global destination for creative freedom.

Our Approach

Where It All Started: A Poem Painted on a House

In 2004, Chinese poet Huang Xiang — who had been imprisoned and tortured for his writing — arrived in Pittsburgh as City of Asylum's first writer-in-residence. He painted his poetry in large Chinese characters across the facade of his new home on Sampsonia Way.

That single, bold act of creative defiance sparked a movement. Other writers followed, other houses were transformed, and gradually an entire neighborhood became a living canvas for free expression.

Today, City of Asylum's placemaking work extends far beyond house publications — it includes public art installations, Alphabet City's cultural hub, the Alphabet Reading Garden, River of Words, and neighborhood revitalization rooted in the belief that art belongs in everyday spaces.

The House Publications

Poetry You Can Walk Through

Our house publications transform ordinary rowhouses on Sampsonia Way into large-scale public art — each one created by an exiled writer and a collaborating artist.

First House Poem

Huang Xiang — House Poem #1

329 Sampsonia Way · Created 2004 · China

The house that started it all. Chinese poet Huang Xiang painted his poems in bold, sweeping calligraphy across the entire facade of his new Pittsburgh home. The characters, visible from down the block, declared what City of Asylum stands for: words will not be silenced.

"My poems were banned in China. Here, they cover a house for everyone to read."

House Publications

308 – 408 Sampsonia Way

Inspired by Huang Xiang's 'House Poem,' additional homes along Sampsonia Way have been transformed into public art installations. Each is a collaboration between a writer and a visual artist, and together they create a walkable outdoor gallery. The house publications have become a Pittsburgh landmark — drawing visitors, inspiring neighborhood pride, and showing how art and human rights advocacy can reshape a community from the ground up.

Public Art

River of Words

In 2014, 140 Northside neighbors each mounted a single word on their home or storefront, creating a half-mile poem honoring City of Asylum's protection of writers. Created by writer-in-residence Israel Centeno and two Venezuelan artists, River of Words remains one of Pittsburgh's most quietly radical public art projects.

Our Placemaking Philosophy

Art-Anchored Community Development

"When you put art at the center of neighborhood development, you create places where people want to be — not just spaces that are economically efficient, but places that are emotionally meaningful."
— Henry Reese, Co-Founder, City of Asylum

Art as Infrastructure

We treat public art not as decoration but as essential infrastructure — the same way a community needs roads and parks, it needs spaces that inspire and connect people.

Writers as Neighbors

Our writers don't live in an ivory tower — they live on ordinary streets, shop at local stores, and become part of the neighborhood. Their presence weaves global perspectives into local life.

Development Without Displacement

We believe neighborhood revitalization should uplift existing residents, not push them out. Our placemaking strengthens community bonds while bringing new energy to the Northside.
